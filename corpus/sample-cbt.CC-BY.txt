Cognitive behavioral therapy, often shortened to CBT, is a structured, present-focused approach
that helps people notice the link between their thoughts, feelings, and behaviors. A central idea
is that unhelpful automatic thoughts and cognitive distortions, such as catastrophizing or
all-or-nothing thinking, can fuel anxiety and low mood. By identifying these thoughts, testing the
evidence for and against them, and practicing more balanced alternatives, people can change how
they feel and act.

A common solution-focused technique is behavioral activation: when someone feels low, they tend to
withdraw from activities, which deepens the low mood. Scheduling small, valued, achievable actions
each day gradually rebuilds momentum and a sense of agency. Goal setting works best when goals are
specific, measurable, and broken into the smallest possible next step.

Managing anxiety often involves graded exposure: approaching feared situations gradually rather
than avoiding them, so the nervous system learns that the feared outcome usually does not happen.
Breathing techniques and grounding exercises can reduce acute physiological arousal in the moment.
